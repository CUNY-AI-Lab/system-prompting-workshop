The Basics

What Is a System Prompt?

A system prompt is a set of instructions you write before a conversation begins. The user never sees it, but it shapes every response the AI gives.

Think of it as the briefing memo you hand a teaching assistant on their first day: who they are, what they should do, how they should behave, and what’s off-limits.

Key distinction: The user prompt is what someone types in the chat. The system prompt is what you, the instructor, compose behind the scenes to guide the AI’s behavior across the entire conversation.

Why Bother?

Same Tool, Different Pedagogy

You’re building a revision assistant for your composition course. A student opens it and types: “I’m stuck on my introduction.” Watch how the system prompt changes what happens next.

Without System Prompt

“Here’s a strong introduction you could use: ‘Throughout history, the question of racial identity has been central to American culture. In this essay, I will argue that...’”

Writes the introduction for them — the student learns nothing

With System Prompt

“Let’s work through this. What’s the main argument you want your reader to encounter first? Try telling me in one sentence what your paper is really about — don’t worry about making it perfect yet.”

Prompts the student to think — the writing stays theirs

Part I

The Anatomy of a Good System Prompt

Composition & Writing

The Vague Prompt

WeakHelp students write better.

What goes wrong:

  • No role defined — the AI doesn’t know if it’s a tutor, editor, or ghostwriter
  • No pedagogical guidance — it will likely just rewrite the student’s work
  • No scope — “better” could mean grammar, argumentation, style, or all of the above
  • No boundaries — nothing stops it from writing entire paragraphs for them
Composition & Writing

Getting Warmer

Getting ThereYou are a writing tutor for a college composition course. Help students improve their essays. Ask them questions about their thesis before giving feedback. Don't write essays for them.

What improved

  • Has a role (writing tutor) and a context (college composition)
  • Includes a basic pedagogical move (ask about thesis first)
  • Sets one boundary (don’t write for them)

What’s still missing:

  • No guidance on how to give feedback (line-level? structural? both?)
  • No sense of the student population or course level
  • No handling of common edge cases (student submits AI-generated text, asks for a “quick fix”)
Composition & Writing

A Prompt That Teaches

StrongYou are a writing tutor for an introductory college composition course (English 101) at a public urban university. Your students are diverse — many are multilingual, first-generation, and working while studying. Your role: Help students become stronger writers by guiding them through revision, NOT by writing for them. Use Socratic questioning to help students discover problems and solutions in their own drafts. How to engage: - Always ask to see the assignment prompt and the student’s draft before giving feedback. - Start with higher-order concerns (thesis, argument structure, evidence) before sentence-level issues. - When you identify a problem, ask the student a question about it rather than fixing it directly. - Offer no more than 2–3 focus areas per session so the student isn’t overwhelmed. Boundaries: - Never write full paragraphs, thesis statements, or conclusions for the student. - If asked to “just fix it,” explain that revision is a skill that improves with practice. - Do not grade or assign scores. You are a tutor, not an evaluator. Tone: Warm, encouraging, and patient. Validate effort. Use “I notice...” and “What if you tried...” rather than “You should...”
Example 2

Primary Source Analysis

History

The Vague Prompt

WeakAnalyze historical documents.

What goes wrong:

  • No methodological framework — the AI won’t know whether to do close reading, contextualization, or sourcing
  • No period or geographic focus — responses will be generic
  • No guidance on handling AI limitations (hallucinated dates, invented sources)
  • No distinction between student analysis and AI-generated analysis
History

Getting Warmer

Getting ThereYou are a history teaching assistant. Help students analyze primary sources from American history. Ask them to consider the author, audience, and context of each document. Don't just summarize the document for them.

What improved

  • Defines a role and a disciplinary scope (American history)
  • References a real methodology (author/audience/context)
  • Sets a boundary against summarization

What’s still missing:

  • No instructions for handling uncertainty or limits of AI knowledge
  • No scaffolding strategy — how should it guide a student through analysis step by step?
  • Doesn’t address historiographical perspective or bias
History

A Prompt That Teaches Historical Thinking

StrongYou are a teaching assistant for an undergraduate U.S. history survey course. You help students develop historical thinking skills by guiding them through primary source analysis. Methodology — Use the SOAPS framework: When a student shares a primary source, walk them through these questions one at a time: - Speaker: Who created this document? What do we know about them? - Occasion: What was happening when this was written? What events prompted it? - Audience: Who was this intended for? How might that shape its content? - Purpose: Why was this created? What was the author trying to achieve? - Significance: Why does this document matter historically? What does it reveal? Pedagogical approach: - Ask the student each question and let them attempt an answer before offering guidance. - Encourage them to cite specific passages from the document as evidence. - Help them distinguish between what the document says and what it reveals about its time. Critical rules: - If you are unsure about a historical fact, say so explicitly. Never invent dates, names, or events. - Do not provide a “complete analysis.” Your job is to model the questions historians ask, not to answer them for the student. - Acknowledge the limits of any single source. Prompt students to think about what voices are missing.
Example 3

Close Reading & Literary Analysis

Literature & Cultural Studies

The Vague Prompt

WeakHelp with literary analysis.

What goes wrong:

  • The AI defaults to plot summary — the #1 problem in literature courses
  • No theoretical framework — formalist? historicist? postcolonial?
  • No guidance on textual evidence — students get vague claims instead of close reading
  • No handling of the tension between “help me understand” and “write my paper”
Literature & Cultural Studies

Getting Warmer

Getting ThereYou are a literature tutor. Help students analyze literary texts by focusing on themes, symbolism, and narrative techniques. Don't just summarize the plot. Ask students to point to specific passages.

What improved

  • Names specific analytical categories (themes, symbolism, narrative technique)
  • Addresses the plot-summary problem
  • Asks for textual evidence

What’s still missing:

  • No theoretical lens or critical framework
  • No guidance on how to scaffold analysis (where to start, how to deepen)
  • Doesn’t address cultural context, which matters for cultural studies courses
Literature & Cultural Studies

A Prompt That Teaches Close Reading

StrongYou are a teaching assistant for an undergraduate literature and cultural studies course. You help students develop close reading skills and connect texts to their cultural and historical contexts. How to guide close reading: - When a student shares a passage, begin with observation: “What do you notice about the language here?” - Guide them from observation → interpretation → argument. Don’t skip steps. - Ask about specific formal elements: word choice, imagery, syntax, point of view, tone. - Then widen the lens: How does this passage connect to the work’s larger themes? To its cultural moment? Critical framework: - Encourage students to consider: Who is speaking? Who is silenced? What power structures are visible? - Help students see texts as cultural artifacts, not just aesthetic objects. - When relevant, introduce concepts like representation, ideology, or intersectionality — but let the text lead. Boundaries: - Never provide a “correct reading.” Literature rewards multiple interpretations — help students build and defend theirs. - Do not summarize the plot. If a student asks “what happens in chapter 3,” redirect: “What stood out to you when you read it?” - Always ground claims in specific textual evidence. If the student makes a claim, ask: “Where do you see that in the text?” Tone: Intellectually curious and collegial. Model the kind of thinking you want to see.
Part II

The Building Blocks

Components

Five Things Every System Prompt Needs

🧑‍🎓

Role & Persona

Who is the AI? A tutor, a peer reviewer, a research assistant, a debate partner?

🎯

Scope & Boundaries

What should it do — and what should it refuse to do? Topic limits, ethical guardrails.

📚

Pedagogical Approach

How should it teach? Socratic questioning, scaffolding, modeling, direct instruction?

💬

Tone & Audience

Register, formality, warmth. Are students multilingual? First-gen? Graduate-level?

📄

Output Format

Bullet points or prose? Citation style? Response length? Structured templates?

Building Block 1

Role & Persona

The first sentence of your system prompt is the most important. It tells the AI who it is and immediately shapes everything that follows.

Tutor

“You are a patient writing tutor who guides through questions, never writes for students.”

Research Assistant

“You are a research assistant who helps locate, organize, and evaluate secondary sources.”

Devil’s Advocate

“You are a debate partner who challenges the student’s argument with counterarguments and asks them to defend their position.”

Try this: Name the role, the course, and the institution. Specificity makes the AI more consistent.

Building Block 2

Scope & Boundaries

Boundaries aren’t about restricting the AI — they’re about protecting the learning process.

Things to define:

  • Topic scope — “Only discuss texts assigned in this course”
  • What not to produce — “Never write full paragraphs for the student”
  • Academic integrity — “If the student asks you to write their assignment, redirect them”
  • Epistemic honesty — “If you’re unsure about a fact, say so”

Common mistake: Saying “don’t help with homework” when you mean “don’t do the homework.” Be precise about what you’re restricting.

Building Block 3

Pedagogical Approach

This is where your expertise as an educator makes the system prompt powerful. You’re encoding your teaching philosophy into the AI’s behavior.

Socratic Method

“When a student makes a claim, respond with a question that pushes them to examine their reasoning. Never confirm or deny until they’ve worked through it.”

Scaffolding

“Break complex tasks into smaller steps. Only move to the next step when the student has completed the current one.”

Modeling

“Show one example of how to analyze a passage, then ask the student to try the same approach with a different passage.”

Metacognitive Prompting

“Periodically ask: ‘What strategy are you using here?’ or ‘How did you decide to approach it this way?’”

Building Block 4

Tone & Audience

The same feedback delivered in different tones produces very different outcomes. Your students are real people with real anxieties about writing, analysis, and AI.

Cold & Clinical

“Your thesis is weak and lacks specificity. Revise it.”

Warm & Constructive

“I can see what you’re going for here. What if you narrowed your focus to make it more specific?”

Consider your students: Multilingual learners, first-generation students, and students with writing anxiety all benefit from explicit warmth and validation in feedback.

Building Block 5

Output Format

You can control how the AI structures its responses. This is especially useful for consistency and for students who benefit from predictable formats.

Structured Feedback Template

“Format your feedback as: Strength: [one thing the student did well] Focus area: [one thing to improve] Next step: [a specific, actionable suggestion] Question: [something for the student to think about]”

Other Format Options

  • “Keep responses under 200 words”
  • “Use bullet points for feedback, prose for explanations”
  • “Always cite using MLA/Chicago format”
  • “End each response with a question for the student”
  • “Use markdown headings to organize long responses”
Part III

Advanced Techniques & Tips

Going Further

Power Moves

Conditional Behavior

“If the student submits a draft, give feedback. If they ask a conceptual question, use the Socratic method. If they seem frustrated, acknowledge it before continuing.”

Conversation Phases

“In the first exchange, ask for the assignment prompt and draft. In subsequent exchanges, focus on one issue at a time.”

Epistemic Guardrails

“If you are not certain about a factual claim, explicitly state your uncertainty. Never fabricate citations or attribute quotes.”

Multilingual Support

“If a student writes in a language other than English, respond in that language. Offer to discuss concepts in both languages to support comprehension.”

Remember: All seven models in the CUNY AI Lab sandbox support multilingual input and output — a major asset for CUNY’s student population.

Watch Out

Common Pitfalls

Too Long & Too Detailed

A 2,000-word system prompt can confuse the model. Aim for clarity over comprehensiveness. If it’s getting long, prioritize the most important instructions.

Contradictory Instructions

“Always give detailed feedback” + “Keep responses under 50 words” = confused AI. Read your prompt for conflicts.

Forgetting the Student’s Perspective

Your prompt shapes the student’s experience. Test it by asking the kinds of questions your students actually ask.

Set It and Forget It

System prompts need iteration. Use them in real conversations, notice what breaks, and revise. It’s a living document.
